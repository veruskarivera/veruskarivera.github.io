Think about a project idea and brainstorm about what features & functions it could have, how technology could be used in new ways, or a better way to do something, a new market or a new user community to serve, an important problem to solve.

Identifying the Problem

The majority of Sign Language speakers understand what other people are trying to
communicate to them by reading their lips. Due to COVID-19, a state-wide mask mandate
was enforced, leaving the ASL community unable to connect with the world around them.

Proposing a Solution

Some people started using clear masks in order to try to help the hearing impaired, but
this was not efficient since these masks are not approved by CDC standards. I started
thinking and I realized that the best way to help was to design an app that would be able
to translate from English to American Sign Language and vice-versa. After proposing
the idea to the group, we all decided that ASL To Go should have learning modules to
also encourage ASL non-speakers to learn the language.

-EMPATHIZE

User Interview | User Research

Since we serve as a learning software for users (American Sign Language  Speakers
and Non-American Sign Language Speakers) 16+, it’s important  that we did research
into the fundamentals that impact learning new  languages. With that being said, the
most significant constraints that ASL  To Go face pertain to linguistic exposure. We
found that the earlier in life  that you start to pick up a new language, the higher the
chances of mastering it are. As ASL To Go is not geared to teach children, but instead
adults, we  face the challenges that come along with language acquisition declining
with age. As designers and content creators, we have to think of innovative  ways to
increase learning motivation.

Outcome

Creating a mobile app UI/UX project from beginning to end was a challenging but rewarding
experience. It was a reminder that the design thinking process is not linear. Through every
phase, I learned something new from the user, with empathy at the forefront. Helping others
feel included is something I’m very passionate about and I was often reminded that I am not the
user. Through surveys, interviews, and the creation of personas, I was able to encompass what
the users wanted out of the app. Testing early in the ideation process was critical because it
helped illustrate pain points the user faced and I was able to rectify those issues quickly, which
saved time later on in the process. Creating a palette and aesthetic for the brand from scratch
was challenging on its own; having no guidelines meant the possibilities were endless. Finding a
palette and typeface that I was content with, while also ensuring it was accessible was pivotal in
ensuring a great user experience.

Future Plans

Based on the information I received from my user testing, I have discovered additional
features that can be useful to implement in future versions of my application. In the next
version, I will focus on adding speech dictation to optimize the user’s time at the moment of
communication. This will be an important feature since ASL To Go could improve
businesses’ accessibility.
